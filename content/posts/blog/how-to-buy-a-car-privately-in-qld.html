How to Buy a Car Privately in QLD

The complete 2026 guide to buying a used car from a private seller in Queensland — what to check, what paperwork you need, and how to protect yourself.

Why Private Sales Are Different in Queensland

When you buy a car from a licensed dealer in Queensland, you get a statutory warranty, a cooling-off period, and consumer protections under the Motor Dealers and Chattel Auctioneers Act 2014.

When you buy privately? None of that applies. No warranty. No cooling-off period. No dealer to go back to. Once you hand over your money and drive away, the sale is final.

That doesn't mean you shouldn't buy privately — some of the best deals are private sales. It means you need to do your homework first.

Step 1: Check the Car's History Before You See It

Before you even message the seller, run these checks:

  • PPSR check — tells you if there's finance owing, if it's been used as security for a loan, or if it's been reported stolen. This is the most important check you can do. A PPSR search costs $2 on the official government site, or you can get a comprehensive report through services like Buying Buddy.
  • Write-off register — shows if the car has been classified as a statutory write-off (can never be re-registered) or a repairable write-off (can be re-registered after inspection).
  • Rego check — verify the registration is current and matches the seller's details.
  • Market value — check RedBook or CarsGuide for a ballpark value so you know if the asking price is fair.

If any of these checks come back with red flags, walk away. There are always more cars.

Step 2: Inspect the Car in Person

When you go to see the car:

  • Meet in a safe, public location during daylight
  • Check the VIN on the compliance plate matches the rego papers
  • Look for panel gaps, mismatched paint, or overspray (signs of unreported repairs)
  • Check all lights, wipers, air conditioning, and electronics
  • Look under the car for oil leaks or rust
  • Check the tyres — uneven wear can indicate alignment or suspension problems
  • Start the engine cold if possible — a warm engine can hide problems
  • Take it for a proper test drive — at least 15 minutes, including highway speeds

Step 3: Get a Pre-Purchase Inspection

If you're serious about the car, pay for a professional pre-purchase inspection (PPI). A qualified mechanic will put the car on a hoist and check everything you can't see from the driver's seat.

A PPI typically costs $150–$300 depending on the provider. That's a fraction of what you'd spend fixing problems you didn't know about.

Step 4: Negotiate the Price

Private sellers expect to negotiate. Here are some tips:

  • Know the market value before you start (RedBook, CarsGuide, or similar listings on Carsales)
  • Point out any issues found during your inspection — these are legitimate reasons for a lower price
  • Don't be afraid to walk away. The best negotiation tool is being willing to leave.
  • If the car needs new tyres, a service, or minor repairs, factor those into your offer

Step 5: Do the Paperwork

In Queensland, you need the following for a private vehicle sale:

  • Safety certificate (previously called a roadworthy certificate) — the seller must provide this. It must be current (issued within the last 14 days when you apply for transfer).
  • Transfer of registration form — both buyer and seller must sign this. You can get the form from Queensland Transport and Main Roads.
  • Receipt of payment — always get a written receipt showing the amount paid, date, and vehicle details.
  • Sale agreement — not legally required in a private sale, but strongly recommended. A written contract protects both parties.

Step 6: Transfer the Registration

You must transfer the registration into your name within 14 days of the sale. You can do this online through the Queensland Transport and Main Roads website or at a customer service centre.

You'll need to pay transfer duty (stamp duty), which is calculated based on the vehicle's market value or purchase price — whichever is higher.

Step 7: Arrange Insurance

Don't drive the car home without insurance. At minimum, get compulsory third party (CTP) insurance, which is included with Queensland registration. But you should also arrange comprehensive or third-party property insurance before you collect the vehicle.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Not checking the PPSR — if there's finance owing, the lender can repossess the car from you, even though you paid for it
  • Paying cash with no receipt — always get documentation
  • Not checking the safety certificate — make sure it's current and genuine
  • Rushing the decision — sellers who pressure you to decide quickly are often hiding something
  • Not getting a PPI — $200 for a mechanic now can save you $5,000 in surprise repairs later

The Bottom Line

Buying privately in Queensland can save you thousands compared to dealer prices. But without the consumer protections that come with a dealer sale, you need to protect yourself. Check the car's history, inspect it properly, get a PPI, do the paperwork right, and transfer the rego on time.
